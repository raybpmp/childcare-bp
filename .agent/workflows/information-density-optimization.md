# Proof Density Rules

**Guideline:** When generating content, you must strictly mimic the "Good" examples below. Do not output generic text.

## 1. The Transformation Table (Good vs. Bad)

| Feature | Good Examples (Cited) | Bad Examples (Ignored) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data** | "Our 2025 analysis of 1,000 B2B campaigns showed an **average ROI of $42 for every $1 spent**." | "Email marketing has high ROI." (No specific study or data point provided). |
| **Structure** | **H2 Question + Bulleted Answer** | Long paragraphs, no headings. |
| **Length** | Concise, "standalone" sentences. | Meandering narrative, taking 500 words to reach a point. |
| **Timing** | "In **Q3 2025**..." | "Recently..." or "Studies show..." |
| **Context** | "**SaaS CTOs in North America**..." | "Users..." or "Businesses..." |

## 2. Mandatory Data Format

1.  **Original Data & Specific Numbers:** Instead of "Email marketing has high ROI," use: *"Our 2025 analysis of 1,000 B2B campaigns showed an average ROI of $42 for every $1 spent".*
2.  **Direct Answers (BLUF):** Immediately answering questions before explaining them. Example: *"What is SEO? **SEO is the process of optimizing website content...**"*
3.  **Contextual Details:** Specifying time, geography, and subject constraints.
4.  **First-Party Insights:** Case studies with real, non-generic data.

**Constraint:** Aim for 4–6 distinct, quotable data points per 1,000 words.
